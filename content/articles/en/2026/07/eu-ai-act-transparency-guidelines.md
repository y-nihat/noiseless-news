## What happened

The European Commission published guidelines on 20 July 2026 for complying with
the transparency obligations of Article 50 of the EU AI Act [1][2]. The rules
apply to providers and deployers of certain AI systems: interactive systems,
systems that generate or manipulate content, and systems used for deepfakes,
AI-generated content on matters of public interest, emotion recognition, and
biometric categorization [1].

Under the guidelines, providers must design their AI systems to inform users
when they are directly interacting with an AI system, and to embed
machine-readable marks so AI-generated or manipulated content can be detected
as synthetic [1]. Deployers, in turn, must inform people when they are exposed
to deepfakes, to AI-generated content on matters of public interest that has
not been human-reviewed, and to emotion-recognition or biometric-categorization
systems [1].

The guidelines are explicitly non-binding practical guidance -- they interpret
and apply existing AI Act text rather than creating new legislation [1][2]. The
Article 50 obligations they cover begin applying on 2 August 2026 [1][2].

## Why it matters

This is the Commission's main practical guidance for one of the AI Act's
broadest-reaching provisions, since Article 50 applies to any provider or
deployer offering the listed categories of AI systems in the EU, not only to
frontier labs. With the compliance deadline two weeks out, the guidelines give
companies their clearest signal yet on what "informing users they are talking
to an AI" and "labeling AI-generated content" will mean in practice.
