## What happened

New York Governor Kathy Hochul signed Executive Order 62 on 14 July 2026,
directing the state Department of Environmental Conservation to pause
discretionary permitting for new data centers that draw 50 megawatts of
power or more — the threshold used for large, "hyperscale" facilities [1][2][3].
The state Department of Public Service will use the pause to prepare a
Generic Environmental Impact Statement assessing the sector's effects on the
power grid, ratepayers, and the environment [1]. The order sets no fixed end
date — it lasts until that review is complete — though the governor's office
and press coverage describe the expected timeline as up to a year [1][2].

The order cites demand driven by artificial intelligence, cloud computing,
and streaming services, and points to roughly 12 gigawatts of data-center
interconnection requests currently pending on New York's grid, as the reason
for the pause [1]. It also directs Empire State Development to publish a
Community Investment Framework within 60 days and creates a state working
group on data center grid interconnection [1].

President Trump criticized the order on Truth Social, calling it a
"terrible decision," writing that data centers are "big, strong, bold, and
Money Machines" and that Hochul acted "for political reasons," and demanded
New York change the policy "immediately" [4]. Hochul publicly defended the
move in response [6]. The Data Center Coalition, an industry trade group,
said the moratorium would divert investment, jobs, and tax revenue to other
states, citing figures that New York's data center sector supported more
than 227,000 jobs and $49 billion of state GDP in 2024 [7].

## Why it matters

This is the first statewide pause of its kind in the US on new large-scale
data centers, coming as AI-driven demand strains regional power grids across
the country [1][2][3]. Because the order affects only new discretionary
state permits above the 50-megawatt threshold — not smaller facilities or
projects already deemed complete — its practical effect will depend on how
long the state's environmental review takes and how permitting resumes once
it concludes [1].
