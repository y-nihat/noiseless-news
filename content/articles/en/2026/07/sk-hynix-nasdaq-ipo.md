## What happened

SK Hynix, the South Korean memory-chip maker, began trading on Nasdaq under
the ticker SKHY on 10 July 2026, after pricing its American depositary
receipt offering at $149 each [1][2]. The deal raised $26.5 billion, the
largest-ever U.S. listing by a foreign company, surpassing Alibaba's $25
billion IPO in 2014 [2][3][4]. It ranks as the second-largest IPO in U.S.
history overall, behind SpaceX's $85.7 billion Nasdaq debut a month earlier,
in June 2026 [3][6]. The ADRs closed their first trading day up about 13%, at
$168.01 [1][5].

SK Hynix says the proceeds will fund a new fabrication plant in Yongin and an
advanced-packaging facility in Cheongju, both aimed at expanding production of
high-bandwidth memory (HBM) — the memory stacked alongside Nvidia's H100,
H200 and Blackwell GPUs to feed data to AI accelerators [2][4]. The company
describes itself as commanding the majority of the global HBM market, though
the precise share varies by quarter and by whether it's measured in shipments
or revenue [4][7].

## Why it matters

The listing is a bet that AI-driven memory demand can sustain valuations built
during the current chip supercycle, giving U.S. investors direct access to a
company whose packaging capacity — not raw chip design — is one of the
tightest bottlenecks in Nvidia's AI GPU supply chain. The $26.5 billion raise
came in below SK Hynix's own earlier target of up to $29 billion, a gap
explained by final book-building rather than softer demand: the offering was
still oversubscribed by roughly seven times the shares on offer [2][4].
Whether the added fab and packaging capacity eases the memory shortage
industry executives have said could last beyond 2030 is a question the IPO
itself doesn't answer.
