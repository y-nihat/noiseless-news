## What happened

China and 29 other countries signed an agreement in Shanghai on 16 July 2026 founding the World AI Cooperation Organization (WAICO), a new intergovernmental body meant to coordinate AI governance and development among its members [1]. Founding signatories include Russia, Brazil, Belarus, Cuba, Venezuela, Pakistan, Indonesia, Kazakhstan and Laos, alongside additional African and Asian states [3][4]. The United States, the European Union and its member states, the United Kingdom, Japan, and South Korea did not join [3][4].

The following day, at the opening of the World AI Conference (WAIC) 2026 in Shanghai, President Xi Jinping referenced WAICO in his keynote, saying "AI development should not be a solo performance by a single country, but a symphony of international cooperation" [2]. He pledged 5,000 AI training and seminar opportunities for developing countries over the next five years, and said China would set up AI cooperation centers with the Association of Southeast Asian Nations, the League of Arab States, the African Union, the Community of Latin American and Caribbean States, the Shanghai Cooperation Organisation, and BRICS [2].

WAICO's founding materials name a headquarters city -- Shanghai -- and a stated purpose, but neither the primary announcements nor independent press coverage reviewed for this article describe a published charter, secretariat, or budget [1][2][3][4]. The concept was first previewed publicly by Chinese Premier Li Qiang at WAIC 2025; the 16 July signing is the first formal, binding commitment by member states.

## Why it matters

WAICO is a concrete, signed alternative to Western-led AI-governance efforts, but its 29-country roster is a China-aligned and Global South coalition rather than a body with broad Western participation -- the absence of the US, EU, UK, Japan, and South Korea is a defining feature of its current membership, not an incidental gap [3][4]. Whether WAICO becomes an operating institution with real authority, or remains a framework agreement, will depend on follow-through -- charter, funding, and staffing -- that has not yet been made public.
