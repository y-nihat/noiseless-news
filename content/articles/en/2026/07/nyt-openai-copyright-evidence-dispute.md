## What happened

On 9 July 2026, the New York Times and four other plaintiffs — the New York Daily News, the Center for Investigative Reporting, The Intercept, and Ziff Davis — filed a motion asking a federal judge to sanction OpenAI in their long-running copyright suit (Case No. 1:23-cv-11195, Southern District of New York, before Judge Sidney Stein) [1][2][3].

The plaintiffs allege that OpenAI spent more than two years telling the court it lacked the technical ability to search its training corpus and ChatGPT output logs for their copyrighted journalism. They say a deposition of OpenAI privacy-engineering lead Vinnie Monaco showed the company had, in fact, already run such searches — including maintaining an internal database of roughly 78 million de-identified ChatGPT conversations, used to gauge its own infringement exposure, that predates the lawsuit [1][3][4]. They further allege OpenAI deleted or made unsearchable billions of chat logs in violation of the court's document-preservation orders, and that a 20-million-conversation sample it did hand over was redacted so heavily as to be "unusable" [1][4].

These are allegations made by one side in active, unresolved litigation — not findings a court has made. OpenAI disputes them directly: spokesperson Drew Pusateri said the claims are "blatantly false," and characterized the motion as an attempt by the Times to access the private conversations of people uninvolved in the case as the underlying lawsuit weakens [1].

## Why it matters

This is a discovery-dispute escalation inside one of the highest-profile AI copyright cases in the US, not a verdict — no court has yet ruled on either the sanctions motion or the underlying infringement claims. If a judge credits the plaintiffs' account, it could shape evidentiary rulings and settlement leverage in a case being watched as a bellwether for how courts handle AI training-data disputes across the news industry.
