## What happened

US District Judge Araceli Martinez-Olguin granted final approval on 20 July 2026 to Anthropic's $1.5 billion settlement of Bartz v. Anthropic, the class-action lawsuit brought by authors over the company's use of their books to train its Claude models [1][2]. It is the largest copyright class-action settlement in US history [1][2].

The judge overruled a set of objections raised by some class members, who argued the deal was too small, gave plaintiffs' attorneys too large a cut, or wrongly excluded certain copyright holders [1][2]. The fund covers more than 480,000 works; after roughly $122 million in attorneys' fees and litigation costs, claimants receive approximately $3,000 to $3,100 per work [2].

The case dates to 2024, when authors sued over Anthropic's practice of downloading pirated books from shadow libraries. Judge William Alsup, who presided before the case's reassignment, ruled in 2025 that using the books to train Claude was fair use, but that Anthropic's retention of more than 7 million pirated copies in a "central library" was not — a ruling that pushed the parties toward this settlement [1][4]. Some rights holders opted out of the class and are pursuing their own separate suits against Anthropic, which remain unresolved [3][4].

## Why it matters

This closes the first major US AI-training copyright case to reach a final, court-approved resolution, out of dozens of similar suits pending against AI companies. The size of the payout and the court's rejection of "too small" objections set an early benchmark other plaintiffs and AI labs will point to as more of these cases reach settlement or trial.
