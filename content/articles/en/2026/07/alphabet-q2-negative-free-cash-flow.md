## What happened

Alphabet reported Q2 2026 free cash flow of −$5,855 million, roughly
−$5.9 billion, according to its earnings release filed with the SEC:
operating cash flow of $39,069 million minus capital expenditure of
$44,924 million [1]. Capital expenditure — spending on property and
equipment — rose from $22,446 million in the same quarter last year to
$44,924 million this quarter, an increase of just over 100% [1][2].

Reuters and the Financial Times both reported that this marks the first
quarter of negative free cash flow in Alphabet's history as a public
company, dating to its 2004 initial public offering [2].

Alphabet ties the higher spending to scaling AI infrastructure and
compute capacity: Google Cloud revenue grew 82% year-over-year, which the
company attributes to enterprise demand for its AI infrastructure and AI
solutions [1][2]. CEO Sundar Pichai said the quarter's results reflect the
company's AI investments "redefining what's possible across every part of
our business" [1]. Alongside the results, Alphabet raised its full-year
2026 capital expenditure guidance to $195–205 billion, up from a prior
$180–190 billion, citing AI and Cloud demand [1][2]. The earnings release
does not break out a separate "AI capex" figure; the $44.9 billion capex
total and the guidance increase cover Alphabet's spending as a whole.

## Why it matters

Alphabet becomes the latest major AI lab to post a financing signal — not
a warning, but a marker — that the current buildout of AI compute is now
large enough to outpace even one of the industry's largest cash generators
in a single quarter. Since capex of this scale is typically funded by cash
reserves, debt, or new financing rather than cut back, this quarter is a
concrete data point in the broader debate over how sustainable the AI
infrastructure spending race is for the companies funding it, and how
investors read cash-flow trade-offs against long-term AI Cloud revenue growth.
