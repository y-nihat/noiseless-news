## What happened

Illinois Gov. JB Pritzker signed Senate Bill 315, the Artificial Intelligence Safety Measures Act (Public Act 104-0538), on 6 July 2026 [1][2]. The law makes Illinois the first US state to require large AI developers to undergo recurring independent third-party safety audits [2][4].

The law applies to "large frontier developers": companies with more than $500 million in annual revenue that build AI models trained using more than 10^26 operations of compute [1][3]. Two sets of obligations phase in on different dates. Starting 1 January 2027, covered developers must file a disclosure statement with the state agency before developing, deploying, or operating a frontier model [1]. Starting 1 January 2028, they must additionally write and publish a "frontier AI framework" addressing catastrophic risk — defined as incidents causing death or serious injury to 50 or more people, or $1 billion or more in property damage — and complete an annual independent audit by a conflict-free third party [1].

Covered developers must also report safety incidents within 72 hours, or 24 hours if there is imminent risk of death or serious injury, to the state agency and the attorney general [2][3]. Violations carry civil penalties of $1 million for a first offense and $3 million for subsequent violations, enforced by the attorney general; the law does not create a private right of action [2][3].

## Why it matters

New York's RAISE Act dropped a similar recurring third-party audit requirement before it was signed, and neither California's SB 53 nor Colorado's AI Act includes one, making Illinois' annual audit mandate a genuine first among enacted state laws [2][4]. The law's practical bite is still two years out: the safety-framework and audit obligations don't take effect until 1 January 2028, leaving room for amendment, legal challenge, or a competing federal framework to arrive first.
