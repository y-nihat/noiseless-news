## What happened

The European Commission issued two decisions against Google on 16 July 2026
under the EU's Digital Markets Act [1][2][3]. The first requires Google to
open core Android capabilities to third-party AI assistants -- including
ChatGPT and Claude -- so they can be invoked and used with the same kind of
system-level access Gemini has today: voice activation similar to "Hey
Google," awareness of on-screen context, and the ability to carry out actions
inside other apps on a user's behalf [2][3]. That requirement takes effect in
July 2027, tied to the next Android release. The second decision requires
Google to share anonymized Google Search query data with competing search
engines and AI chatbot providers, starting January 2027 [1][2].

Google objected. Kent Walker, President of Global Affairs at Google and
Alphabet, said in a company statement that "today's decisions risk
undermining vital privacy and security guardrails for millions of Europeans,"
adding that opening system-level Android permissions to outside apps
"threatens device security by granting external apps sensitive and powerful
device permissions without these safeguards," and that sharing search data
would expose "Europeans' private searches... to unfamiliar companies, without
adequate anonymisation of the data and without user knowledge or consent"
[1].

## Why it matters

This is a direct regulatory intervention into how AI assistants compete on
the world's dominant mobile operating system: it would give rivals to
Google's own Gemini the kind of on-device reach Google currently reserves for
itself, and hand AI search products a stream of Search query data that has
so far stayed inside Google. Whether the remedies land as designed depends on
implementation details the decisions themselves don't fully settle -- how
"comparable access" is measured, and how anonymization is verified -- which
is exactly the kind of dispute that tends to follow major DMA rulings.
